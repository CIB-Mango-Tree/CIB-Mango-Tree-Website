The Story of CIB Mango Tree

By Ben Sando, project manager at CIB Mango Tree

Around May in Taiwan, mangoes start to weigh down tree branches, dense and golden. They are Taiwan’s signature fruit and a symbol of the island. But Taiwan has earned another reputation: a vigorous defender of internet freedom and open online discourse through civic action.

Founded in June 2024, the CIB Mango Tree project pays homage to the grassroots Taiwanese civic tech projects that have resisted manipulation of the internet. Technology like a Cofacts — a crowdsourced fact-checking bot — have been built from communities of committed citizens. While CIB Mango Tree is based in Washington, DC, our team of volunteers are replicating the spirit and activism of our friends in Taiwan.

CIB Mango Tree is a free and open-source research project designed for researchers, journalists, and watchdogs that want to monitor social media for signs of coordinated inauthentic behavior (CIB). CIB refers to the use of networks of fake social media accounts, often in large numbers, to simulate real human discourse and create the illusion that certain talking points are more popular than they really are. CIB can drown out real human voices and replace them with arguments about health, politics, or the environment that serve the interests of a narrow few. Sometimes, companies are even contracted to manipulate online discussions with large networks of inauthentic accounts.

Analyzing social media activity at scale requires computational tools. Unfortunately, many motivated citizen watchdogs lack the technical ability to program these analysis tools themselves. CIB Mango Tree software aims to bridge this skills gap by giving subject matter experts access to technology that does this computational analysis for them. Our analysis tools are built in an easy-to-use application, so watchdogs don’t need any technical skill at all to use it.

Our first computional tool examines whether accounts are posting identical phrases again and again — a clear sign of bot activity. It’s simple enough to grasp, but the tool requires relatively sophisticated code based on natural-language processing. Other tools in our toolkit leverage the time of day when accounts post content to determine if they are acting in coordination, and more.

Equipped with computational methodologies, watchdogs can raise awareness of social media manipulation using hard evidence from big data. They can lobby their local leaders, write articles, or press social media companies to take more action to prevent manipulation. This will be especially handy during moments of animated public discussion when manipulation can cause serious damage, like elections, natural disasters, and disease outbreaks.

That’s why we designed our first analysis tools to be easy to understand and use. Simply put, they look for the “low-hanging fruit” of CIB, like verbatim repeated phrases or accounts that post at the exact same time. If you can discover this kind of activity, why resort to a more complicated test that other citizens might not understand?

After they have reached for the “low-hanging fruit,” the CIB Mango Tree project guides users towards more sophisticated tests, the “mid- and high-hanging fruit.” In doing so, we provide a framework for CIB analysis that allows even novice watchdogs to monitor and protect free and open internet discourse.

At CIB Mango Tree, anyone can join us and support our work, whether through software development, graphic design, stakeholder engagement, or any other skill set. You can join our CIB Mango Tree channels on the Tech DC Slack community, and attend Civic Tech DC project nights to meet us in person.

We are fighting for a healthy, clean, and organic internet — just like a mango. Whether near or far, we hope you’ll support us as we grow.
